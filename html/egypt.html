On Egypt

Pashons 4th, 1736

Ptolemy II Philadelphus in front of "El Bibliotheca" in Alexandria.

Egypt, Egypt, Egypt! Why Egyptians talk Egypt all the time? I guess everybody here in the western world must have heard about the curse of the pharaohs. It is neither a myth nor a work of fiction. Mostly all Egyptians know pretty well that they will be cursed if they leave their homeland, and so was I.

To whichever god decreed this flight: Have mercy, bring me home!
Surely you will let me see the place in which my heart dwells!

What is more important than that my corpse be buried in the land in which I was born?

Come to my aid! What if the happy event should occur?

May god pity me! May he act so as to make happy the end of one whom he punished!
May his heart ache for one whom he forced to live abroad!

If he is truly appeased today, may he hearken to the prayer of one far away!

May he return one whom he made roam the earth to the
place from which he carried him off!

By Sinuhe, c. 1800 BC, in Canaan.

1    What is so Special about Egypt?

1.1    Quick Introduction

Let's start with the basics. This continent is called Africa:

The north-eastern (top-right) corner is known as Egypt. Let's zoom in:

It is important to note that Sinai, Israel, and their neighbours are considered as part of Asia, not Africa. That being said, all of Egypt, except Sinai peninsula, lies in Africa (i.e. Egypt is transcontinental). Now it's crystal clear that North Africa is nothing but a big desert, called Sahara (formally called El Saharaa El Kubra, or The Greatest Desert). The Sahara has no food and no water. Nobody lives there.

If this is the case, then why would anybody live in Egypt, which takes up the eastern part of this Sahara? To answer this question, let's zoom in again:

Now it should be clear to you that Nile Valley, Nile Delta, and Faiyum, are green enough for people to live in. But, why? Why this part, out of all the Sahara of North Africa, is especially green? we still didn't answer the core question: What is special about Egypt?

1.2    Formation of the Nile Valley

60 million years ago, during early Palaeogene, a number of rifts have formed in Northeast Africa, thanks to the activity of Plate Tectonics in that area, which is still ongoing as of the day of writing this HTML page. This ongoing activity has resulted in:

  • The formation of a ditch in the Egyptian land. This ditch has in turn divided the Egyptian land into three parts:
    1. The Western Desert;
    2. The Nile Valley; and
    3. The Eastern Desert.

  • The formation of the Red Sea as well as the mountain chains that run along both sides of the sea:
    1. Egyptian Itbay; and
    2. Arabian Hijaz.
